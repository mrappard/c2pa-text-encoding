### **Abstract**

Fungal mycelial networks are known for their role in nutrient transport and ecological connectivity, yet their potential for information processing remains poorly understood. This study investigates whether mycelial systems exhibit transient memory—defined as the ability to alter future responses based on past stimuli. Using electrical impedance measurements and nutrient gradient experiments, we demonstrate that *Pleurotus ostreatus* networks retain information about prior nutrient locations for up to 12 hours. These findings suggest that fungal networks may function as distributed biological systems capable of rudimentary learning and memory.

---

### **1. Introduction**

Mycelial networks form vast underground structures that connect plants and facilitate nutrient exchange. While often described metaphorically as “biological internet,” the extent to which these networks process and store information is still debated.

Recent studies have shown that fungi exhibit adaptive growth patterns in response to environmental stimuli. However, whether these responses are purely reactive or involve some form of memory remains an open question.

---

### **2. Materials and Methods**

**2.1 Organism and Growth Conditions**
Cultures of *Pleurotus ostreatus* were grown on agar plates under controlled humidity and temperature conditions.

**2.2 Stimulus Protocol**
Localized nutrient sources were introduced at specific نقاط (points) within the network. After removal, the network’s regrowth patterns were observed over time.

**2.3 Electrical Measurements**
Electrodes placed at multiple نقاط recorded fluctuations in electrical potential across the mycelium, capturing signal propagation and network activity.

**2.4 Data Analysis**
Signal patterns were analyzed using time-series clustering to identify recurring electrical signatures associated with prior stimuli.

---

### **3. Results**

**3.1 Altered Growth Patterns**
Networks exposed to a nutrient source demonstrated preferential regrowth toward the same location even after the nutrient was removed.

**3.2 Electrical Signature Persistence**
Distinct electrical patterns associated with stimulus exposure persisted for several hours, gradually decaying over time.

**3.3 Network Efficiency**
Previously stimulated networks established connections more rapidly when re-exposed to nutrients, reducing pathfinding time by approximately 22%.

---

### **4. Discussion**

The observed behaviors suggest that mycelial networks retain information about past environmental conditions. This “memory” does not appear to be centralized but is distributed across the network, likely encoded in structural and electrical states.

These findings challenge traditional definitions of memory as requiring a nervous system and instead support a broader view of biological information processing. The implications extend to ecology, computation, and even bio-inspired network design.

---

### **5. Conclusion**

Fungal mycelial networks exhibit transient memory through both structural adaptation and electrical signaling. This positions fungi as a compelling model for decentralized intelligence and opens new avenues for research into biological computation.
