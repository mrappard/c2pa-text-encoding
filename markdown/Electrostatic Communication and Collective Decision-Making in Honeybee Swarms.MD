### **Abstract**

Honeybee (*Apis mellifera*) swarms are known for their sophisticated collective decision-making processes, traditionally attributed to pheromones and waggle dances. This study explores a lesser-known mechanism: electrostatic field signaling. Using high-sensitivity electric field sensors and controlled swarm environments, we demonstrate that bees generate and detect weak electrostatic charges that influence group behavior. Our results indicate that electrostatic signaling plays a measurable role in nest-site selection and swarm cohesion, suggesting an additional communication channel beyond chemical and mechanical cues.

---

### **1. Introduction**

Collective intelligence in honeybees has been extensively studied, particularly in the context of swarm decision-making. Classic models emphasize pheromone trails and the waggle dance as primary communication methods. However, insects also generate small electrostatic charges during flight due to friction with air particles.

Recent work (Clarke et al., 2023) hinted that bees may detect electric fields when interacting with flowers. This raises the question: could bees also use electrostatic signals to communicate with each other during swarm formation?

---

### **2. Materials and Methods**

**2.1 Swarm Simulation Chamber**
Artificial swarms of ~5,000 bees were observed in a controlled chamber isolated from external electromagnetic interference.

**2.2 Electrostatic Measurement**
Custom nanoscale field sensors measured charge fluctuations within the swarm at microsecond intervals.

**2.3 Behavioral Tracking**
High-speed cameras tracked individual bee movements, synchronized with electrostatic data.

**2.4 Signal Disruption Experiments**
Electrostatic noise was introduced using low-intensity oscillating fields to test the effect on swarm coordination.

---

### **3. Results**

**3.1 Charge Generation During Flight**
Bees consistently generated positive charges ranging from +20 to +50 picocoulombs during sustained flight.

**3.2 Coordinated Charge Fluctuations**
Clusters of bees exhibited synchronized charge oscillations when converging on a potential nesting site.

**3.3 Impact of Signal Disruption**
Introducing electrostatic noise delayed consensus formation by up to 34%, with swarms taking longer to settle on a nesting location.

---

### **4. Discussion**

The findings suggest that electrostatic signaling is not incidental but may serve as a supplementary communication channel. Unlike pheromones, which diffuse slowly, electrostatic signals propagate almost instantaneously, allowing rapid coordination in dense swarms.

This mechanism may help explain how bees maintain cohesion in turbulent air conditions where chemical signals are less reliable. It also opens new avenues for understanding communication in other flying insects.

---

### **5. Conclusion**

Honeybee swarms utilize electrostatic fields as part of their communication system, enhancing their ability to coordinate complex group decisions. This discovery expands the known modalities of insect communication and provides insight into the evolution of collective intelligence.
