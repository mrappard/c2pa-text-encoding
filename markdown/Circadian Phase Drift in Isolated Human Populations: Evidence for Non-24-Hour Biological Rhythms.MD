**Title:**
**Circadian Phase Drift in Isolated Human Populations: Evidence for Non-24-Hour Biological Rhythms**

**Authors:**

### **Abstract**

Human circadian rhythms are commonly assumed to align with the 24-hour day. However, under conditions of temporal isolation, endogenous rhythms may deviate from this cycle. This study examines circadian phase drift in individuals deprived of external time cues over a 30-day period. Using continuous monitoring of melatonin levels, core body temperature, and sleep-wake cycles, we find that the intrinsic human circadian period averages 24.6 hours. Participants exhibited progressive phase delays, resulting in significant misalignment with the external day-night cycle. These findings have implications for shift work, space travel, and sleep disorders.

---

### **1. Introduction**

The human circadian system regulates sleep, metabolism, and hormonal activity. While it is entrained to the 24-hour day by environmental cues such as light and social interaction, the intrinsic period of the circadian clock is not perfectly synchronized with this cycle.

Early experiments suggested a slight deviation, but methodological limitations left the precise value uncertain. This study revisits the question using modern measurement techniques in a fully controlled environment.

---

### **2. Materials and Methods**

**2.1 Participants**
Twenty-four healthy adults (ages 20–35) were recruited and screened for sleep disorders.

**2.2 Isolation Protocol**
Participants were housed in a time-isolated facility with no access to clocks, natural light, or external communication. Artificial lighting was kept constant.

**2.3 Physiological Monitoring**
Melatonin secretion was measured via salivary samples every two hours. Core body temperature was continuously recorded using ingestible sensors.

**2.4 Data Analysis**
Circadian phase was determined using peak melatonin onset and temperature minima. Drift rates were calculated relative to a 24-hour reference.

---

### **3. Results**

**3.1 Intrinsic Circadian Period**
The average endogenous circadian period was 24.6 ± 0.2 hours across participants.

**3.2 Phase Drift**
Participants exhibited a consistent daily phase delay of approximately 36 minutes, leading to complete desynchronization over the study period.

**3.3 Behavioral Effects**
Sleep onset times shifted progressively later, accompanied by reduced sleep quality and mild cognitive impairment during misaligned phases.

---

### **4. Discussion**

The results confirm that the human circadian system is not inherently synchronized to a 24-hour cycle but requires environmental cues for alignment. The observed drift aligns with prior theoretical models of biological oscillators.

These findings are particularly relevant for individuals in environments lacking natural time cues, such as submarines, polar research stations, or long-duration space missions. They also provide insight into conditions like delayed sleep phase disorder.

---

### **5. Conclusion**

Human circadian rhythms naturally exceed 24 hours, leading to phase drift in the absence of external cues. Understanding this intrinsic timing mechanism is essential for managing sleep health in modern and extreme environments.
